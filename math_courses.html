Differentiable Manifolds and Lie Groups (Semester 7)

This course covered the following topics: Definition of smooth manifolds, partitions of unity, Whitney embedding theorem, Sard's theorem. Vector bundles, tangent and cotangent bundles. Flows, Lie derivatives, Frobenius theorem. Lie groups, Lie algebras, exponential map. Tensor fields, alternating tensors, differential forms, exterior derivative, integration, Stokes' theorem, De Rham Cohomology. Riemannian manifolds.

Probability Theory (Semester 7)

This course covered the following topics: Probability measures and randown variables, pi and lambda systems, expectation, the moment generating function, the characteristic function, laws of large numbers, limit theorems, conditional contribution and expectation, martingales, infinitely divisible laws and stable laws.

Algebraic Topology (Semester 6)

This course covered the following topics. Homology : Singular homology, excision, Mayer-Vietoris theorem, acyclic models, CW-complexes, simplicial and cellular homology, homology with coefficients. Cohomology : Cohomology groups, relative cohomology,cup products, Kunneth formula, cap product , orientation on manifolds, Poincare duality.

Topics in analysis (Semester 6)

This was a topics course, which means it dealt with a wide variety of problems not found in any single analysis course. Some of the topics that were covered are: Wierstrass' and Fejer's theorems, the theorem of Muntz and Szasz, Chebyshev's approximation, moment problems in one dimension, connection to orthogonal polynomials, tridiagonal matrices, continued fractions, Hall's marriage theorem, Dilworth's theorem, proof of existence and uniqueness of Haar measure on compact topological groups, proof of isoperimetric inequality via Brunn-Minkowski inequality and via symmetrization, isoperimetric inequalities for the sphere and the Gaussian measure.

Introduction to Homotopy Type Theory (Semester 6)

This was an introductory course in homotopy type theory, which aims to serve as an alternative foundation for mathematics. The course covered the first 5 chapters of the Homotopy Type Theory Book.

Complex Analysis (Semester 6)

This course covered the following topics: Holomorphic functions, Cauchy’s integral formula, power series, Liouville’s theorem, maximum-modulus theorem, Isolated singularities, residue theorem, the Argument Principle, real integrals via contour integration, Mobius transformations, conformal mappings, Schwarz lemma, automorphisms of the disc, Normal families and Montel’s theorem and Riemann mapping theorem.

Measure and Integration (Semester 6)

This course covered the basics of measure theory, and integration on measure spaces. The Lebesgue measure was constructed, and the basic results of integration theory like Monotone Convergence theorem, Dominated convergence theorem, etc. were proved.

Algebra II (Semester 6)

This course focused on Galois theory, and in the later half, commutative algebra from Atiyah-Macdonald's commutative algebra.

Introduction to Algebraic Topology (Semester 5)

This course covered the basics of the Fundamental Group, as well as some covering space theory, including the Galois Theory of Covering Spaces. The latter half of the course also covered higher homotopy groups and computed some of them for higher spheres using Hopf fibration.

Topology (Semester 5)

This course covered the basics of Topology, as well as few of the fundamental results, i.e. Tychonoff's Theorem, Tietze Extension Theorem, Urysohn's Metrization Theorem, and Baire Category Theorem, and after developing the machinery of the Fundamental Group, Brouwer's Fixed Point theorem for dimension 2 was proven.

Algebra I (Semester 5)

The first half of this course covered group actions in some detail: Burnside's lemma was proven, and so were the Sylow Theorems. The second half of this course was an introduction to commutative algebra: we dealt with modules over commutative rings. We also saw how the tensor product interacts with the Hom functor.

Linear Algebra (Semester 5)

The course dealt with canonical forms, i.e. Jordan and Rational canonical forms; the latter was developed via the characterization theorem for modules over PIDs. The course also looked at bilinear and quadratic forms, as well as spectral theory for real operators.

Multivariable Calculus (Semester 5)

The first half of the course dealt with differentiation and the inverse function theorem, and its various corollaries, e.g. the implicit function theorem, Morse lemma, etc. The second half dealt with integration; partitions of unity, riemann integrals over compact sets, change of variables, differential forms, exterior algebra, Stokes' Theorem, and a look at de Rham cohomology.

Functional Analysis (Semester 5) (Audited)

In this course, we started off with infinite dimensional Hilbert and Banach spaces, and proved Uniform Boundedness Theorem, Hahn-Banach Theorem, and the open mapping theorem. In the second half of the course, we moved on to general topological vector spaces, and the theory of compact operators.

Basic Analysis (Semester 4)

This course covered some foundational material, i.e. axiomatic set theory and ZFC, following which it quickly developed the analysis we'd seen prior to the course. The latter half of the course focused on function spaces, and proved Ascoli's theorem, and Stone-Weirstrass theorem.

Algebra and Number Theory (Semester 4)

This course focused on Elementary Number Theory in the first half, and in the second half, quickly changed focus to Algebra, i.e. developed group, rings and fields, and proved the Primitive Root Theorem, and the Fundamental Theorem of Algebra.

Ordinary Differential Equations (Semester 4)

This course dealt with existence and uniqueness of solutions to ODEs given appropriate hypotheses. It also looked at systems of ODEs and proved qualitative results about the solutions to these systems, i.e. the Stable Manifold Theorem.

Probability and Statistics (Semester 3)

This was an introductory course in Probability theory, and covered material upto the Central Limit Theorem, and Law of Large Numbers.

Introductory Analysis and Linear Algebra I and II (Semester 1 and 2)

This was a two semester long introductory sequence. The first half focused on single variable analysis, with an additional goal of getting the students used to writing proofs. The second half had linear algebra, which covered a fair bit of material, including the spectral theorem for self adjoint operators. It also covered partial derivatives and total derivatives, and a little bit of ordinary differential equations, i.e. power series method, etc.
